Sub: Request to withdraw the proposed project for 'Holistic Development of Great Nicobar Island in Andaman & Nicobar Islands' by NITI Aayog

Most Respectfully,

The Great Nicobar Island, harbouring one of the richest repositories of biodiversity in the whole South and Southeast Asia, might soon be history, as the Indian government works its way around on a contentious project, called "Holistic Development of Great Nicobar Island". The ambitious initiative aims to turn the mostly remote and untouched Great Nicobar Island into a transshipment port and tourism hotspot. This petition is in regard to the potential severe impacts of the proposal, if not paid attention to and checked immediately.

1. The Great Nicobar Island is a sheer treasure trove of biodiversity, consisting of Biosphere Reserve, Tribal Reserve, National Parks and Wildlife Sanctuaries. This island is perhaps one of the last untouched tropical evergreen rain forests of the world, with endemic fauna and flora much of which is undocumented and unknown to the scientific community. The Island covers an area of 910 sq. of which 865.84 (95.2%) is densely forested, within which is the tribal reserve for the Aboriginal Shompen Tribe comprising 751.40 sq. kms. Two national parks, as part of the biosphere, the Galathea National Park (GNP) is spread over 107.10 sq. kms and the Campbell National Park (CNP) comprising 391.75 sq. kms. Hence, there is a very strong case to leave this last bastion of nature and biodiversity untouched in its pristine form for future generations. However, the proposed project cuts through the biosphere and apportions 70.6 sq.km into the project area

2. Great Nicobar Island is inhabited by aboriginal Shompen and Nicobarese, Shompen are approximately 237 and the Nicobarese 1,094 individuals. Though the Nicobarese have joined mainstream, the Shompen Tribes still prefer living in the forests and continue their lives as aboriginal people, living deep within forests, as indigenous tribes world over, in their traditional manner (can be explained in greater detail if required, however Government instrumentalities have extensive knowledge and know the necessity of protecting and preserving the indigenous people in their own lands/forests).

3. At present, 95.2% of the Great Nicobar Island is under pristine forest cover. The proposed project envisages slicing an area of 166 sq. kms. from the total 910 sq. kms which amounts to 18.25% of the Island's total land mass being diverted for the project. The proposed megaproject envisages 8.5 lakhs trees will be felled and 300 hectares of land reclaimed from dredging up the ocean.

4. A very serious consideration is the role of rainforests being the carbon sinks of the world, and essential to the control of Co2 emissions needed to restrict warming of the planet. In view of the seriousness with which the world is pursuing transitions from fossil fuels to clean energy to cut carbon emissions/ global warming, if India proceeds with deforestation on a massive scale, the country would be deemed as one of the doublespeak, posingon global forums, and doing the opposite in its country. a negative image like that must be avoided under all circumstances.

5. Research has found that deforestation can heat a local area by as much as 4.5°C and can even increase temperatures in undisturbed forests as far away as 6km. This research used satellite data over Indonesia, Malaysia, and Papua New Guinea (which fall in similar temperature zones as with other ASEAN countries). Rise in ocean surface temperatures in Nicobar are therefore imperative at the scale of deforestation this project proposes, which would affect the El Nino currents which originate in the Indian ocean area. As is well known, the El Nino impacts rainfall on the Indian peninsula, putting an existential threat on the mainland with skewed rainfall patterns and an increased intensity and frequency of extreme as well as erratic weather events.

6. However, effective forest management practices could increase the relevance of forests as a carbon sink, buying time for the switch to green energy sources even though the rapid decarbonization of the global economy is still necessary. The risk of losing native forests is quite significant, and it includes the loss of the indigenous people's livelihoods that depend on their surrounding environment. Without sufficient protective measures, environmental wrongdoing will start to erode the barrier created by indigenous territory and other protected places.

The claimed ‘Holistic’ Development by Govt. of India, MoEFCC fails in as being neither holistic or all encompassing; as it violates the fundamental Environmental Laws Rules and Regulations of the country, preserving the Great Nicobar Biosphere, Protection of National Parks of Great Nicobar, protecting endemic and endangered Wildlife of Great Nicobar, and encroaching on Reserved Forest of indigenous tribes and against the Tribal Policy of Shompen Tribe of Great Nicobar;

8. Hence the 'Holistic Development of Great Nicobar Island in Andaman & Nicobar Islands' is against nature, indigenous tribes, endangered and endemic flora and fauna and pursues a solely economic agenda, and therefore deserves to be scrapped forthwith and the Great Nicobar Island left in its pristine condition without any further anthropogenic interventions.
